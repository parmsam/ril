Jake Luigi's central framework: before attacking, (1) name the worst-case scenario if it fails, (2) identify the key indicators that tell you whether that outcome is likely, and (3) adjust aggression accordingly — attack hard when indicators are favorable, stay conservative until they are. The goal isn't to avoid risk but to attack only when failure is already unlikely. Grip fighting is the clearest application: a grip that can't move the opponent, stop their movement, or reposition you is a security blanket, not an advantage — win the grip fight first, then commit.

A related idea: control before concentration. Isolating a single limb too early gives up overall control in exchange for finishing power. Better to control multiple limbs (diagonal control — one shoulder, opposite hip — to eliminate rotation) before narrowing onto one target. Many escapes are rotational, so if an opponent can still rotate, they still have options.

Jake also borrows from Josh Waitzkin the idea of deliberate universal language: instead of thinking "arm triangle," think "concentrating resources onto one target while giving up broader control." Domain-specific vocabulary traps learning inside the sport; universal concepts — risk management, reducing variability, establishing advantageous constraints — transfer directly to investing, engineering, and decision-making, multiplying reps far beyond mat time.
