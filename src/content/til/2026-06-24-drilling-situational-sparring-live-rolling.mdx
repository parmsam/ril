A common framing is that each training stage "confirms" the previous one, but that undersells what each stage actually develops:

- **Drilling** → skill acquisition. Build the motor pattern with a cooperative partner until the movement stops requiring conscious thought. Goal: *can I perform the movement correctly?* The metric isn't reps — it's whether you can just do the movement. Chasing rep counts is the wrong optimization; a sloppy hundred reps grooves the wrong pattern.
- **Situational sparring** → skill application. Start from the relevant position, with increasing resistance. Learn timing, entries, and what to do when the first attempt fails. Goal: *can I recognize the opportunity and execute against resistance?*
- **Live rolling** → skill integration. Everything is live. You must first arrive at the position, choose between multiple techniques, and respond to someone actively trying to win. Goal: *can I consistently use this technique within my overall game?*

That last transition is where techniques that felt solid in positional work disappear — because you haven't yet learned how to reach the position, when to choose it over another option, how to chain it with other attacks, or how to recover when it fails. These are separate skills that only live rolling reveals.

This matches motor learning research: drilling builds the motor pattern; situational sparring builds perception, timing, and resistance execution; live rolling builds decision-making, adaptability, and retention under chaos. It's why many high-level coaches spend a disproportionate amount of class time on positional sparring — it's the bridge, not the destination.
