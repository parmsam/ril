Tom Yeh puts it directly: "Whether you own something, you value something — it's actually proportional to how much time you spend on acquiring that piece of knowledge." Buying a degree, earning a certificate, or prompting AI all produce the artifact without the ownership.

What he cares about as an educator isn't whether students remember the transformer equations a year later — he expects they won't. It's that they once *tried*: stayed in the library for hours, attempted to open the black box. That willingness is what differentiates someone who invested from someone who didn't. The specific content fades; the self-knowledge that you *can* learn something hard doesn't.

This is close to the IKEA effect, but with a stronger claim: it's not just that you value what you built — it's that you *know* what you built in a way you can't fake or shortcut.
