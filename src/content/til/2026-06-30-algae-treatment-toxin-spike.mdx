When oxidizing agents like hydrogen peroxide are applied to an algae bloom before species identification is complete, they rupture cell membranes in a process called cell lysis. If the bloom turns out to be cyanobacteria rather than non-toxic green algae, lysing the cells releases internal toxins — microcystin, anatoxin-A — directly into the water, spiking concentrations in the hours immediately after treatment even as the bloom appears to be dying.

The same cell destruction that causes the toxin spike also destroys the morphological features scientists use to identify species under a microscope. Treat first, characterize later means the evidence of what was actually in the water may be gone by the time anyone looks. In the Lincoln Memorial Reflecting Pool case, workers poured hydrogen peroxide before formal identification was completed; a later examination found the samples difficult to characterize because the chemicals had already degraded the cells.

Warm, shallow, nutrient-rich, stagnant water is equally hospitable to non-toxic green algae and to cyanobacteria — the conditions don't distinguish between them. Professional harmful algal bloom management protocol calls for species confirmation before chemical intervention specifically because the treatment decision and the public health risk are completely different depending on which organism is present.
